*** She identified three main problems with coops, and similar organizations:

- free-rider problem:: when benefits are shared but costs of contribution are individual.
  Egotistical persons under-contribute and over-extract.
  In a cooperative, this is the member who consumes services, votes for benefits, but skips meetings, dodges work obligations, or under-invests.

- commitment problem:: even when members agree on rules, each has an ongoing incentive to defect when nobody is watching.
  Promises today don't bind behavior tomorrow.

- mutual monitoring problem:: solving the first two requires monitoring,
  but monitoring is itself costly and is itself a collective good;
  so who pays to watch the others?

- supply problem (the meta-problem) :: Ostrom's sharpest insight is that even if you knew the perfect rules.
  Creating the institution that enforces them is itself a second-order collective action problem.
  Someone has to invest time and political capital to design rules, recruit members, and bootstrap legitimacy.
  And that investment is a public good for everyone who later benefits.
  This is why cooperatives are hard to start even when they would obviously be better than the alternative.

*** Failure modes she documents in the case studies:

Across her cases (Turkish fisheries, Sri Lankan irrigation, Nova Scotia inshore fisheries, California groundwater basins, etc.), the recurring failures are:

- Unclear or contested boundaries :: when you can't say who's a member and who isn't, or what resource the cooperative controls, free-riding becomes uncontrollable.
- Mismatch between rules and local conditions :: rules imposed from outside (or copied from another context) that don't fit the actual ecology, technology, or culture of the group.
- Lack of graduated sanctions :: cooperatives that have only "warn" and "expel" tend to under-enforce because expulsion feels disproportionate; without intermediate steps, violations accumulate.
- No cheap conflict-resolution forum :: disputes that should be small become existential because there's no quick, low-cost way to settle them.
- External authorities not recognizing the cooperative's right to make its own rules :: the state overrides, ignores, or undermines local governance, so members can't trust their own agreements will hold.
- Failure to nest :: single-tier cooperatives that try to handle everything from local enforcement to regional coordination collapse under the load. Polycentric, nested layers are needed.
- Information asymmetries and scientific uncertainty :: members making decisions without good data about resource state, or with conflicting expert claims.

*** Self-governance is ont free.
Costs Ostrom is honest about even when cooperatives work

She's clear that self-governance is not free.
The recurring costs are: time (deliberation is slow), transformation costs of changing rules as conditions change, monitoring and enforcement costs that members must absorb, and information costs of staying current on resource conditions and on each other's behavior. Her argument isn't that cooperatives avoid these costs — it's that under the right design principles, members internalize them more efficiently than markets or states would.


*** graduated sanctions
Good question — this gets at the practical heart of Ostrom's fifth design principle (graduated sanctions). She doesn't give a universal ladder, but synthesizing across her case studies and the broader cooperative governance literature, here's the typical range of intermediate steps between "warn" and "expel."

- Informal, low-visibility steps (before anything formal) :: Quiet word from a peer or elder;
  someone the violator respects mentions it privately. Ostrom emphasized that in well-functioning commons, most enforcement never escalates past this stage because members internalize norms. Public visibility of the behavior at member meetings — not naming and shaming, but making aggregate compliance data visible so deviation becomes socially uncomfortable.

- Formal warnings, escalating in weight :: Verbal warning logged but not published.
  Written warning placed in the member's record.

- Public warning :: read at a general assembly or posted.
  Each step raises the social cost without yet touching economic rights.

- Restorative steps (make-the-resource-whole) :: Required restitution
  the violator repairs the damage, replaces what was taken, contributes extra labor, or pays into a common fund.
  This is heavily used in Ostrom's irrigation and fisheries cases because it directly restores the resource and signals seriousness without permanent harm.
  Mandatory mediation or facilitated dialogue with affected members.
  A required service contribution above the baseline (extra monitoring shifts, extra maintenance work).

- Economic sanctions (graduated by magnitude) :: Small fines, scaling with severity and repetition.
  Ostrom's Turkish fishery case (Alanya) used escalating fines tuned to local norms.
  Forfeiture of a specific benefit for a defined period: loss of a turn in a rotation, loss of access to a particular zone, loss of patronage refund or dividend for the period.
  Withholding of profit-share or returns until violation is cured.

- Governance and participation sanctions :: Loss of voting rights for a defined period.
  Loss of eligibility to hold elected office.
  Removal from committees or working groups.
  Required probationary status with closer monitoring and reporting obligations.
  These hit identity and standing inside the cooperative without removing the person economically.

- Suspension (the step right before expulsion) :: Time-limited suspension of membership rights;
  typically with a defined path back: complete X obligations, attend Y sessions, demonstrate Z behavior over a probation window.
  Suspension is what makes expulsion proportionate, because by the time you expel, the member has already had a clear bounded chance to return.

- Expulsion (last resort) :: With or without buyout of equity, depending on the cooperative's bylaws and the jurisdiction's coop law.

Two design notes Ostrom is insistent about

Sanctions must be applied by the members themselves or by people accountable to them, not by external authorities — otherwise the legitimacy collapses and members stop reporting violations. And the ladder must be calibrated to local stakes: a fine that's trivial in a wealthy cooperative is devastating in a subsistence one. The Alanya fishers, the Valencia huertas, the Swiss alpine villages — each calibrated their own ladder to what hurt enough to deter without destroying.

For an LGSC-structured cooperative in Mexico, the bylaws (bases constitutivas) need to spell this ladder out explicitly because Mexican cooperative law gives wide latitude on internal discipline but requires that sanctions be defined ex ante and applied with due process. If you want, I can sketch how a graduated-sanctions ladder might look for Contracted Intelligence Coop specifically — where the "resource" being governed is something like code quality, contract compliance, or shared client relationships rather than a fishery.

** Good now what do we do with that?

I think the solution for the problems Ostrom identified lies in a Liquid Coop Organization.

A Liquid Coop is one where the shares of the members and their distribution changes over time.

So for example the 1st month, the month with the most risk
3,643 shares are minted.
Once a month the members get a question: Who of this two people helped the coop succeed the most this month?
And you are given two names.
You can select A,B,C or don't know.
And do it again.
By the end you have a list sorted by people who helped the coop succeed.
Then the shares printed that month get allocated according to the order in the list. Following a function that starts high, has high wide section and then drops assymptotically.
The idea is to reward the people that contribute the most higher, then most people make almost the same shares, and the people at the bottom that are perceived to contribute the least.
Are given the least shares.

Next month only 97% of the original shares are minted so: 3643*.97 = 3533
This "reflects" the fact that the company actually has less risk the 2nd month of opeartion. [fn:4]
And so on, the goal is to reward with shares people staying in the cooperative, but the people that started the thing get a lot more rewards given that the risk was high.

** How does a liquid organization addreess Ostrom's problems:

- free-loading :: if a free-loader doesn't cooperate they will get 0 shares that month.
  Which would be a clear indication that they don't contribute or are not perceived as contributeding.
  So they can use that mechanism as feedback to improve, or leave.

- mutual monitoring :: The monthly poll is automated, it is comparision based (so less cognitive overload).
  And it doesn't actually evalute it just a sorting of who is perceived to have contributed the most,
  and who is perceived to have contributed the least.
  Where most is actually kind of the same contribution.

- supply problem (the meta-problem) :: In this case the solution is that people that started the coop get a lot more shares that the person that joins on the 120th month (10th year).
  When it would seem that you are going to succeed.

** OK so it avoids the most common problems but does it bring benefits?

Let's imagine several cases:

- The organization survives over 10 years one of the founders leaves:: For a good chunk of time the founder still receives, dividents from their shares.
  But slowly but surely, since his shares are getting devaluated, the dividends diminshi. Showing that the founder is no longer contributing.

- A new member joins in year 10th and now only 100 shares are minted each month :: Correct, this new mempber has joined a less risky organization.
    Now in order to make from the coop they will have to stay for many years, in a stable organization, accruing ownership doesn't look so bad.

- A founder just worked the 1st six months and then left the company :: in that case the founder would have lot's of shares.
      Is it unfair tho? it seems they worke hard during the riskier times.
      So maybe they receive a reward.

- On the 5th year the coop receives an acquisiton offer, for a billion dollars :: then allocation of shares get's counted.
  the percentage of ownership of the company is established and the billion dollars is split accordingly.

- We want a contractor but don't have money to pay them :: We can pay them in shares that month!
  So that their contribution becomes part of the history of the coop.
  And they get dividends when those are split.
  or could even get a good payday if the coop were to be acquired.

** Stocksy United

Stocksy is a marketplace that sells high quality photographs and videos.
Around a thousand photographers each month upload photos.
Customers that require a professional photo for their marketing materials, ads or presentations.
Now the licensing of the photo is quite interesting and let me simplify it a little bit:

/I'm making this numbers up just as indicative, the idea is the important thing/

It costs less than $100 USD to get one photo, for one material (ad, flyer, etc.) for one customer.
$200 USD if you want to use the photo for any material.
$300 USD if you want all of your marketing team to be able to use the photo, and
$400 USD if you want exclusive rights to the photo, meaning no one else can buy it.

Now the interesting part is how they split the profits:

50% of each sell goes to the photographer.
Then the remaining 50% is split this way:
5% goes for the investors[fn:1].
15% goes for the people operating the page. And
30% goes to all the photographers split equally. Not by sales but by being part of the platform.

Now observe how this split motivates 3 behaviors:

1. It is good for everyone to sell photos, not *only* your photos but *any* photo.
2. Every time they accept a new photographer they know they are taking slimmer slices of a hopefully bigger pie.
   So the new member, rather be good.
3. They help each other become better photographers. It is in the best interest of everyone to have better photos to sell.

*** Results so far
From 2014 to 2022, Stocksy has paid $50.1 million in royalties to artist.

** So what is the alternative?

Other models of companies closer to a software companies are:

- Lawyer firms that have a pyramid of Equity Partners, Salaried Partners (Senior lawyers with prestige but no equity in the company).
- Hollywood Model. A production company is more like a coordinator of independent contractors (script writers, actors, directors). That only come together to make the movie.
  And then disband until the next one.

So (some) software engineers are more like expert lawyers, and actors... how can we manage them?

A Cooperative.
In cooperative the employees are the owners of the company.
In that sense they are more like partners in a lawyer firm than workers in an assembly line.

The advantage of that is that as a Lawyer firm you can *attract* and *maintain* the best talent (engineers).
